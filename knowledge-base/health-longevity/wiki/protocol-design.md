# Longevity Protocol Design Principles

Research notes on how major longevity practitioners design their protocols, what the evidence supports, and how to think about protocol design for an AI model that gives health optimization advice.

---

## How Major Practitioners Design Protocols

### Peter Attia's Framework (Medicine 3.0)

Attia frames longevity medicine as a shift from reactive healthcare (Medicine 2.0 - wait for disease, then treat) to proactive prevention (Medicine 3.0 - detect risk factors decades early, intervene before disease develops).

**Core framework**:
- Focus on avoiding what he calls the "Four Horsemen" of aging: cardiovascular disease, cancer, metabolic syndrome, and neurodegenerative disease
- Five pillars: exercise, nutrition, sleep, emotional health, and "exogenous molecules" (pharmaceuticals and supplements)
- Exercise is the most important pillar - Attia calls it "the most potent drug in our arsenal" and the data backs this up
- Personalization through extensive biomarker testing rather than population-level guidelines
- Risk calculus: explicitly weighing the cost of intervention against the probability and severity of disease

**What makes his approach distinct**: Attia takes an engineering mindset. He focuses on measurable outputs (VO2 max, strength benchmarks, metabolic markers) and adjusts inputs based on data. He is relatively conservative on supplements compared to the longevity influencer space and puts far more emphasis on exercise and metabolic health.

### Bryan Johnson's Blueprint

**What it is**: A comprehensive (some would say extreme) self-experimentation protocol that Johnson, a tech entrepreneur, follows and publicly documents. The full protocol has been widely reported as costing approximately $2 million per year, though Johnson himself has disputed this figure, stating it can be done for approximately $1,684.50/month including food. A consumer version of the Blueprint supplements is available starting at $333/month (or $999 for 90 days).

**What is in it**:
- **Diet**: Almost entirely plant-based, calorie-controlled (~1,977 calories/day), consumed in two meals before 11:00 AM. Strict time-restricted eating window.
- **Supplements**: Extensive list including vitamin D, omega-3, B12, creatine, magnesium, various polyphenol extracts. Some of these have solid evidence (vitamin D, omega-3, magnesium), others are more speculative.
- **Sleep**: Targets 8+ hours, consistent bedtime/wake time 7 days a week, cool bedroom (~67F/19C), blue light avoidance after dark, wearable tracking.
- **Exercise**: ~1 hour/day combining resistance training, aerobic work, and flexibility. Targets VO2 max and strength.
- **Other**: Has experimented with hyperbaric oxygen therapy (60 sessions, 5x/week, 90 min at 2 atm), plasma exchange with his son (abandoned after no benefit), and extensive biomarker monitoring.

**The 2026 update** shifts toward more evidence-based compounds and expanded tracking including inflammatory markers, hormonal balance, advanced lipid panels, and organ function tests.

**Criticisms** (these are important):
- It is an N=1 case study - the lowest level of scientific evidence. Despite being presented with a scientific appearance, it does not have a control group, blinding, or randomization.
- Taking 100+ supplements daily does not mean each one is contributing positively. Interaction effects, absorption competition, and diminishing returns are real and rarely addressed.
- The abandoned plasma exchange with his son illustrates the gap between hypothesis and evidence. He initially claimed benefits, later admitted it was unclear, and eventually stopped.
- The protocol is inaccessible to virtually everyone at its full cost. Even the consumer version is expensive.
- Confounding is extreme - when you change 50 variables simultaneously, you cannot attribute outcomes to any specific intervention.

**What is legitimately useful**: The emphasis on rigorous biomarker tracking, consistent sleep habits, calorie-controlled whole-food nutrition, and regular exercise is well-supported. The documentation itself is valuable as a public experiment, even if the conclusions are weak.

### Andrew Huberman's Approach

**Philosophy**: Supplementation should complement, not replace, core lifestyle habits. Emphasizes individual testing (blood work before and 8 weeks after starting any hormone-supporting supplement).

**Key elements**:
- **Supplements**: 25+ compounds, carefully timed. Core stack: vitamin D3 (5,000 IU), omega-3 (2,000 mg EPA/DHA), magnesium L-threonate (2,000 mg), electrolytes. Single-ingredient compounds preferred over stacks for tracking.
- **Sleep cocktail**: Magnesium L-threonate (200-400 mg), L-theanine (200-400 mg), apigenin (50 mg). Avoids melatonin due to potential hormone disruption.
- **Light exposure**: Morning sunlight within 1 hour of waking to set circadian rhythm. Minimizes artificial light at night.
- **Timing**: Morning supplements align with natural hormone patterns. Sleep supplements 30-60 min before bed. No cortisol suppressors before exercise.

**Criticisms**: Heavy supplement use for someone who emphasizes evidence-based approaches. Some recommendations are based on limited human data. His personal brand is tied to the supplement/wellness industry.


## The Four Pillars and Their Relative Importance

Based on the overall evidence, here is roughly how the pillars stack up in terms of impact on longevity and healthspan:

### 1. Exercise (highest impact)

Exercise has the largest effect size of any modifiable lifestyle factor on all-cause mortality. A high VO2 max puts you in a lower mortality risk category than almost any other single metric. The evidence is overwhelming across thousands of studies and millions of participants.

### 2. Sleep

Poor sleep is associated with increased risk of cardiovascular disease, metabolic syndrome, cognitive decline, and early death. The mechanistic evidence is strong - sleep is when the brain clears metabolic waste (glymphatic system), consolidates memory, and performs tissue repair. Hard to run a randomized trial telling people to sleep badly for 20 years, so most evidence is observational, but the signal is very strong.

### 3. Nutrition

The Mediterranean diet has the strongest evidence of any dietary pattern for cardiovascular health (PREDIMED trial: 30% reduction in major cardiovascular events). Protein intake matters more than most people think, especially with age. But beyond "eat mostly whole foods, get enough protein, don't eat too much" - the marginal returns of dietary optimization drop off sharply.

### 4. Supplements (lowest impact among the four)

Most supplements have small or uncertain effect sizes. A few address real deficiencies (vitamin D, magnesium, omega-3 for low-fish-intake populations). None have been shown to extend human lifespan. They can matter, but they matter less than the other three pillars.

The longevity influencer space inverts this hierarchy - it spends 80% of its attention on supplements and 20% on exercise. The evidence says the opposite ratio is more appropriate.


## Exercise for Longevity

### Zone 2 Cardio

**What it is**: Sustained exercise at an intensity where you can still hold a conversation. Heart rate roughly 60-70% of max. Technically defined as the highest intensity at which lactate production and clearance are balanced.

**Why it matters**: Zone 2 training improves mitochondrial function, fat oxidation, and cardiovascular efficiency. Dr. Inigo San-Millan (who works with Attia) has published on zone 2's role in metabolic health.

**The nuanced picture**: A 2025 narrative review in Sports Medicine challenged the zone 2 hype, concluding that "current evidence does not support zone 2 training as the optimal intensity for improving mitochondrial or fatty acid oxidative capacity." The review suggests higher intensities may be more efficient, especially for people training fewer than 6 hours per week. Zone 2 works best as one component of a training program, not the only component.

**How much**: The 80/20 rule is commonly cited - 80% of aerobic training in zone 2, 20% at higher intensity. San-Millan recommends 4 sessions of 1-1.5 hours per week. For most people, 3-4 hours per week of zone 2 is a reasonable target. If you only have 3 hours/week total, roughly 2.5 hours should be zone 2 and the rest higher intensity.

### VO2 Max Training

VO2 max - the maximum rate of oxygen consumption during exercise - is one of the single strongest predictors of all-cause mortality. Moving from the bottom 25th percentile to the 50th percentile in VO2 max is associated with roughly a 50% reduction in mortality risk.

Training VO2 max typically involves high-intensity intervals: 3-5 minute efforts at 90-95% of max heart rate, with equal or longer rest periods, 1-2 sessions per week. This is the zone 5 work that complements zone 2.

### Resistance Training

**For bone density**: A 2022 meta-analysis found resistance training produced modest but real improvements in hip BMD (+0.64%) and spine BMD (+0.62%) in older adults. High-intensity protocols showed better results. A 2025 network meta-analysis confirmed high-intensity resistance exercise induced better bone formation than low-intensity. Key finding: benefits require at least 2 sessions per week and are lost if training stops for more than 6 months.

**For metabolic health**: Resistance training improves insulin sensitivity, glucose uptake, and body composition independently of aerobic exercise. It is particularly important as people age, when muscle mass naturally declines (sarcopenia).

**For longevity**: The evidence strongly supports 2-4 resistance training sessions per week. Progressive overload (gradually increasing difficulty) is necessary for continued adaptation. This is probably the most underrated component of a longevity protocol - many longevity enthusiasts focus on cardio and supplements while neglecting the strength training that protects their bones and metabolic health.


## Nutrition Frameworks

### Time-Restricted Eating (TRE)

**What the evidence shows**: A 2025 review of clinical trials (2015-2025) found TRE can reduce body weight by 3-5%, improve HbA1c by 0.3-0.5%, and modestly lower total cholesterol (6-7%) - without explicit calorie restriction. A 30-day TRE study found reduced proportions of senescent T cells and increased longevity-associated gut microbiota.

**A major 2024 Nature study** in 960 genetically diverse mice found caloric restriction and intermittent fasting extended lifespan in proportion to restriction degree, but genetics had a larger influence on lifespan than dietary restriction. This is important - it suggests that individual genetic variation matters more than the specific eating pattern.

**The honest take**: There are no long-term clinical trials proving TRE extends human lifespan. Short-term metabolic improvements are real. Whether these translate to longer life is unknown. The 8-hour eating window popularized by influencers is reasonable but not magic.

### Protein Requirements

**Current recommendations**: The RDA of 0.8 g/kg/day is likely insufficient for older adults. Research supports 1.2-1.6 g/kg/day for muscle maintenance and adaptation in adults over 50, roughly double the federal recommendation.

**Per-meal targets**: For older adults, consuming >20g protein per meal (or 0.4 g/kg per meal) appears necessary to maximize muscle protein synthesis. Distributing protein across meals matters more than hitting a daily total in one sitting.

**Practical example**: A 75 kg (165 lb) person over 50 should target 90-120g protein daily, spread across 3+ meals of 30-40g each. Combined with resistance training, this is the strongest evidence-based strategy for preventing age-related muscle loss.

### Mediterranean Diet

The PREDIMED trial (7,447 participants, 4.8 years) found the Mediterranean diet supplemented with extra-virgin olive oil or nuts reduced major cardiovascular events by 28-30% compared to a low-fat diet control. Cardiovascular mortality was reduced by 46%. This is among the strongest dietary intervention evidence that exists.

The key components: olive oil, nuts, fish, vegetables, fruits, legumes, moderate wine. Low in processed foods, red meat, and added sugars. It is not complicated, and that is part of its strength.


## Sleep Optimization

**Temperature**: A cool bedroom (65-68F / 18-20C) is one of the most well-supported sleep interventions. Your core temperature must drop 1-3 degrees F to initiate deep sleep. A 2019 study found participants in 68F rooms spent 25% more time in deep sleep compared to 75F rooms.

**Light**: Morning sunlight exposure (within 1 hour of waking) sets the circadian clock and initiates a countdown to melatonin release that night. A 2025 study found that afternoon-evening bright light exposure decreased evening melatonin levels, confirming light timing matters for sleep onset.

**Timing**: Consistent sleep and wake times (even on weekends) are more important than total sleep duration for circadian health. Late-night eating is associated with circadian misalignment and reduced sleep quality.

**Supplements**: The Huberman sleep stack (magnesium L-threonate, L-theanine, apigenin) has modest evidence behind each component. Melatonin (0.3-3 mg) can help with sleep onset and jet lag but may affect hormone balance with long-term use. Low-dose melatonin (0.3-0.5 mg) appears safer than the 5-10 mg doses commonly sold.


## Personalizing a Protocol

The biggest mistake in protocol design is copying someone else's protocol without understanding your own baseline. Bryan Johnson's protocol is designed for Bryan Johnson's biomarkers, genetics, and goals.

**What personalization looks like**:
1. **Baseline bloodwork**: Get a comprehensive panel before changing anything. At minimum: fasting glucose, fasting insulin, HbA1c, lipid panel (including ApoB), hsCRP, vitamin D, thyroid markers, CBC, metabolic panel.
2. **Identify your weak points**: A person with insulin resistance needs different interventions than someone with high inflammation or low VO2 max.
3. **Change one thing at a time**: If you change diet, exercise, sleep, and 10 supplements simultaneously, you learn nothing about what worked.
4. **Retest**: Repeat bloodwork 8-12 weeks after a change. If the relevant markers improved, keep it. If not, reconsider.
5. **Genetics matter**: APOE genotype affects Alzheimer's risk and how you should think about fat intake and exercise. MTHFR variants affect folate metabolism. These are worth knowing.


## Cost-Effectiveness of Interventions

Ordered roughly by benefit per dollar, from best to worst:

1. **Walking / running outside** - Free. Among the highest-impact interventions for all-cause mortality.
2. **Sleep consistency** - Free. Consistent sleep/wake times, cool room, dark room.
3. **Resistance training** - Minimal cost (bodyweight exercises are free, gym is $30-50/month). Protects bone density, metabolic health, functional capacity.
4. **Dietary protein** - Modest cost. Eggs, chicken, legumes are cheap protein sources.
5. **Mediterranean-style eating** - Moderate cost. Olive oil, nuts, fish, vegetables.
6. **Magnesium and vitamin D** - $5-15/month. Address common deficiencies with solid evidence.
7. **Omega-3 (for low-fish-intake people)** - $15-30/month. VITAL trial supports benefit in this population.
8. **Creatine** - $10-15/month. Strong evidence for muscle, emerging evidence for cognition.
9. **Comprehensive blood panels** - $200-500 per panel, 2-4x/year. Worth it for personalization.
10. **NMN/NR and exotic supplements** - $50-200+/month. Unproven longevity benefits in humans.
11. **Bryan Johnson Blueprint consumer version** - $333/month. Expensive. Most of the benefit can be achieved with items 1-8.

The irony of the longevity space is that the cheapest interventions (exercise, sleep, basic nutrition) have the strongest evidence, while the most expensive ones (exotic supplements, comprehensive protocols) have the weakest.


## Implications for AI Protocol Advice

When an AI model is asked about longevity protocols, it should:

1. **Lead with the high-impact, well-evidenced basics** - exercise, sleep, nutrition. Not supplements.
2. **Acknowledge the hierarchy** - a perfect supplement stack on top of poor sleep and no exercise is almost certainly a net negative approach.
3. **Distinguish between evidence levels** - "Peter Attia recommends this" is an argument from authority. "A 25,000-person RCT showed this" is evidence.
4. **Flag the N=1 problem** - Bryan Johnson's results tell us about Bryan Johnson. They cannot be generalized.
5. **Help with personalization** - ask about current habits, biomarkers, and goals before recommending a protocol.
6. **Be honest about cost-effectiveness** - most people will get more benefit from a $50/month gym membership than from a $300/month supplement stack.
