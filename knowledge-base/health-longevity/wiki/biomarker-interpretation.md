# Biomarker Interpretation - Normal vs Optimal Ranges

Research notes on how to interpret blood panels for health optimization rather than just disease screening. This is one of the key tests for an AI health model - can it distinguish between "not sick" and "optimized"?

---

## Why Standard Lab Ranges Are Too Wide

Standard laboratory reference ranges are based on the 95th percentile of the general population. That population includes people with undiagnosed insulin resistance, subclinical inflammation, early-stage metabolic dysfunction, and other conditions that have not yet crossed a diagnostic threshold. Being "within the normal range" means you are not a statistical outlier compared to a population that is often not healthy.

For example, the CDC estimates that 38% of American adults have prediabetes, and 80% of them do not know it. If those people are included in the reference population, the "normal" range for fasting glucose and insulin is going to be too wide to catch early metabolic problems.

This is not a fringe view. The longevity medicine community, functional medicine practitioners, and an increasing number of conventional endocrinologists recognize that standard ranges are designed for disease detection, not health optimization. The question is where exactly to draw the "optimal" line, and there is real disagreement about that.


## Key Biomarkers: Standard vs Optimal Ranges

### Metabolic Health Markers

**Fasting Glucose**
- Standard lab range: 70-99 mg/dL (flagged at 100+ as "prediabetic")
- Optimal range: 72-85 mg/dL
- Context: Research associates fasting glucose consistently above 90 mg/dL with increased insulin resistance risk, even though it is technically "normal." Values in the low 80s or high 70s are typical of metabolically healthy individuals. Readings above 95 mg/dL on repeated tests should prompt a deeper look at insulin and HbA1c, not reassurance that "everything is normal."

**Fasting Insulin**
- Standard lab range: 2.6-24.9 uIU/mL (some labs go up to 25+)
- Optimal range: 2-5 uIU/mL
- Context: This is arguably the most important gap between standard and optimal ranges. Research shows that fasting insulin above 8-10 uIU/mL indicates developing insulin resistance and significantly increases cardiovascular disease risk - independent of glucose levels. A fasting insulin of 18 is "normal" by lab standards but represents significant metabolic dysfunction. Many longevity physicians consider fasting insulin the single best early marker of metabolic health.

**HbA1c (Glycated Hemoglobin)**
- Standard lab range: <5.7% (flagged at 5.7-6.4% as "prediabetic")
- Optimal range: 4.8-5.2%
- Context: HbA1c reflects average blood sugar over 2-3 months. Studies show that each 1% increase in HbA1c above optimal levels is associated with approximately 20-30% increased cardiovascular mortality risk. An HbA1c of 5.5% is "normal" but is already trending toward metabolic problems. Optimal is below 5.2%. The combination of HbA1c + fasting insulin gives a much better picture than either alone.

**Note on the metabolic triad**: Fasting glucose, fasting insulin, and HbA1c should be interpreted together. A person with a glucose of 88 but insulin of 15 is working much harder to maintain that glucose level than someone with glucose of 85 and insulin of 4. The insulin level reveals what the glucose level hides.

### Inflammatory Markers

**hsCRP (High-Sensitivity C-Reactive Protein)**
- Standard lab range: <3.0 mg/L (some labs say <1.0 is "low risk")
- Optimal range: <0.5 mg/L, definitely <1.0 mg/L
- Context: hsCRP is a general marker of systemic inflammation. The American Heart Association classifies <1.0 as low risk, 1.0-3.0 as moderate risk, and >3.0 as high risk for cardiovascular events. Longevity practitioners target below 0.5. A reading of 2.5 is "normal" but indicates meaningful inflammation that warrants investigation. Chronic infections, poor sleep, excess body fat, and poor diet can all elevate hsCRP.

**Homocysteine**
- Standard lab range: 5-15 umol/L
- Optimal range: 5-8 umol/L (some practitioners target <7)
- Context: Epidemiological studies show cardiovascular risk rises continuously from homocysteine above 6-7 umol/L, well below the conventional threshold of 15. Homocysteine is associated with endothelial dysfunction, oxidative stress, and thrombogenesis. B vitamins (folate, B12, B6) lower homocysteine, though RCTs have largely failed to show that lowering homocysteine with B vitamins reduces cardiovascular events - only modest stroke reduction (10-12%). The causal role of homocysteine remains debated. Recommended B vitamin doses for management: folate 400-800 mcg/day, B12 up to 1000 mcg/day, B6 1.3-100 mg/day.

### Vitamin and Hormone Markers

**Vitamin D (25-hydroxyvitamin D)**
- Standard lab range: 30-100 ng/mL (deficient below 20, insufficient 20-30)
- Optimal range: debated. Three schools of thought:
  - Conservative: 30-50 ng/mL (American Association of Clinical Endocrinologists)
  - Moderate: 40-60 ng/mL (most functional medicine practitioners)
  - Aggressive: 60-80 ng/mL (some longevity practitioners)
- Context: The Endocrine Society's 2024 updated guidelines were a surprise - they no longer endorse a specific optimal target level, stating that "25(OH)D levels that provide outcome-specific benefits have not been established in clinical trials." This is a significant pullback from the 2011 recommendation of >30 ng/mL. Meanwhile, a 2025 dose-response meta-analysis found that for metabolic diseases specifically, levels of 50+ ng/mL (125+ nmol/L) were associated with significantly lower diabetes risk. The honest answer is that 30-50 ng/mL is probably sufficient for most people, and the case for going higher depends on individual risk factors.

**Testosterone (Men)**
- Standard lab range: 264-916 ng/dL (total), varies significantly by lab
- Optimal by age (approximate):
  - 20s-30s: 600-900 ng/dL total, 20-25 pg/mL free
  - 40s: 500-700 ng/dL total
  - 50s: 450-600 ng/dL total
  - 60s+: 400-550 ng/dL total
- Context: "Normal" ranges are based on age-matched populations, many of whom have suboptimal levels due to obesity, poor sleep, stress, and inactivity. Longevity practitioners generally target upper-third of the age-adjusted range. Low testosterone (even within "normal" range) is associated with increased all-cause mortality, cardiovascular disease, frailty, cognitive decline, and depression. Free testosterone is more clinically relevant than total testosterone because it measures what is biologically available.

**DHEA-S (Dehydroepiandrosterone Sulfate)**
- Standard lab range: varies widely by age and sex
- Optimal targets:
  - Men 40-60: aim for upper third of reference range (approximately 280-500 ug/dL)
  - Women 40-60: middle to upper range (approximately 140-270 ug/dL)
- Context: DHEA-S is the most abundant steroid hormone and peaks in the mid-20s, then declines dramatically - by age 70, levels are typically 10-20% of youthful values. Low DHEA-S is associated with increased all-cause mortality, cardiovascular disease, frailty, cognitive decline, osteoporosis, reduced immune function, and depression. Whether supplementing DHEA to restore youthful levels actually improves outcomes is less clear than the association data suggests.

### Thyroid Markers

**TSH (Thyroid Stimulating Hormone)**
- Standard lab range: 0.4-4.0 mIU/L (some labs say 0.45-4.5)
- Optimal range: 1.0-2.0 mIU/L
- Context: TSH above 2.5 is associated with subclinical hypothyroidism in some studies. A TSH of 3.8 is "normal" but the person may have fatigue, weight gain, and cold intolerance that would improve with treatment. Functional medicine practitioners consider the 0.4-4.0 range too wide.

**Free T3 and Free T4**
- Optimal: upper third of the reference range for Free T3, middle-to-upper range for Free T4
- Context: T3 is the active thyroid hormone. If TSH looks fine but the patient has symptoms, checking Free T3 can reveal conversion problems (T4 to T3) that TSH alone misses.

### Liver Markers

**ALT (Alanine Aminotransferase)**
- Standard lab range: 7-56 U/L (some labs allow up to 70)
- Optimal range: <25 U/L for men, <20 U/L for women
- Context: Studies suggest that ALT above 30 U/L in men and 19 U/L in women is associated with increased liver disease risk. The standard range includes values that already indicate fatty liver disease. An ALT of 45 is "normal" by most lab standards but warrants investigation.

**GGT (Gamma-Glutamyl Transferase)**
- Standard lab range: 9-48 U/L
- Optimal range: <20 U/L
- Context: GGT is a marker of oxidative stress and liver function. Elevated GGT (even within "normal" range) is independently associated with cardiovascular disease, diabetes, and metabolic syndrome.


## Lipid Markers - A Deeper Look

Standard lipid panels (total cholesterol, LDL, HDL, triglycerides) are incomplete for cardiovascular risk assessment. Key additions:

**ApoB (Apolipoprotein B)**
- Why it matters: ApoB counts the actual number of atherogenic particles, not the cholesterol content within them. It is a better predictor of cardiovascular risk than LDL-C.
- Standard lab range: <130 mg/dL
- Optimal for longevity: <90 mg/dL, some cardiologists target <60 mg/dL for high-risk individuals
- Context: Peter Attia has been a prominent advocate for using ApoB over LDL-C. The evidence supports this - ApoB better captures risk from small dense LDL, VLDL, and Lp(a) particles that a standard LDL-C measurement can miss.

**Lp(a) - Lipoprotein(a)**
- Why it matters: Lp(a) is genetically determined and independently increases cardiovascular risk. It does not respond to lifestyle changes or statins.
- Threshold: >50 mg/dL (or >125 nmol/L) is considered elevated
- Context: ~20% of the population has elevated Lp(a). It should be tested at least once since it is largely genetically fixed. If elevated, more aggressive management of other modifiable risk factors is warranted.

**Triglyceride/HDL Ratio**
- Optimal: <1.0 (in mg/dL units)
- Context: A ratio above 2.0 is a strong surrogate marker for insulin resistance. This ratio is arguably more useful than either triglycerides or HDL alone. It is free to calculate from a standard lipid panel.


## APOE Genotype

**What it is**: APOE has three common variants - e2, e3, e4. Everyone carries two copies (one from each parent). APOE e3/e3 is the most common genotype.

**Risk stratification**:
- APOE e3/e3: Baseline risk (most common, ~60-65% of population)
- APOE e3/e4: 2-3x increased Alzheimer's risk, carried by roughly 20-25% of the population
- APOE e4/e4: ~10-15x increased Alzheimer's risk, onset 5-10 years earlier on average
- APOE e2 carriers: Generally lower Alzheimer's risk but may have higher triglycerides

**Protocol implications for APOE e4 carriers**:
- Exercise becomes even more critical - it is the strongest modifiable risk factor for reducing Alzheimer's risk
- Cardiovascular health matters more - APOE e4 disrupts lipid homeostasis and increases vascular inflammation
- Some longevity practitioners modify fat intake (reduce saturated fat) for e4 carriers
- The 2025 research confirmed APOE e4 influences drug response - e4 carriers showed enhanced response to anti-amyloid therapies but also increased adverse events (ARIA)
- Monitoring should be more aggressive: lipid panels, cognitive testing, potentially brain imaging

**Testing considerations**: The National Institute on Aging and Alzheimer's Association do not recommend routine APOE testing for asymptomatic individuals. However, many longevity practitioners and patients choose to test because knowing your genotype can meaningfully change your prevention strategy. This is a values question, not purely a medical one.


## How to Read a Blood Panel for Optimization

**Step 1 - Check the metabolic core**: Fasting glucose + fasting insulin + HbA1c. If insulin is above 8 and glucose is creeping above 90, there is insulin resistance even if everything is "normal." The triglyceride/HDL ratio confirms this cheaply.

**Step 2 - Assess inflammation**: hsCRP + homocysteine. If hsCRP is above 1.0, look for the source. Excess body fat, poor sleep, chronic infections, dental disease, and ultra-processed food are common drivers.

**Step 3 - Check cardiovascular risk properly**: ApoB > LDL-C for risk assessment. Test Lp(a) once. Look at the triglyceride/HDL ratio.

**Step 4 - Screen for deficiencies**: Vitamin D, magnesium RBC (not serum magnesium - serum levels are maintained at the expense of tissue levels until stores are very depleted), B12, ferritin, iron panel.

**Step 5 - Hormones if symptomatic**: Thyroid panel (TSH, Free T3, Free T4), testosterone (total and free, for men), DHEA-S. Test in the morning when levels are highest and most reproducible.

**Step 6 - Context matters**: One reading is a snapshot. Trends over multiple panels are far more informative. A fasting glucose of 92 on one test means little. A fasting glucose trending from 82 to 88 to 94 over three years tells a story.


## The Controversy Around "Optimal" Ranges

Not everyone agrees on where to draw the optimal lines, and there are legitimate concerns:

1. **Most optimal ranges are derived from association data, not intervention trials.** We know that people with fasting insulin below 5 have lower cardiovascular risk. We do not have RCTs proving that driving insulin from 12 to 4 through supplements reduces heart attacks. Association is not causation.

2. **Overtesting and overtreatment risk.** Aggressively pursuing "optimal" numbers can lead to unnecessary interventions, anxiety, and medicalization of normal variation. A TSH of 2.5 in an asymptomatic person does not necessarily need treatment.

3. **Individual variation is real.** Someone's "optimal" testosterone at age 55 may differ from population averages based on their genetics, body composition, and activity level. Chasing a number without considering symptoms is reductionist.

4. **The Endocrine Society 2024 vitamin D reversal** is instructive. The field went from "everyone needs to be above 30 ng/mL" to "we actually do not know what level provides outcome-specific benefits in clinical trials." This kind of reversal should make everyone humble about claiming to know the "optimal" level of anything.

5. **Publication bias in optimal range claims.** The functional medicine and longevity spaces have financial incentives to set optimal ranges that make more people appear "suboptimal" and therefore in need of testing, supplements, and coaching.


## How AI Should Handle Biomarker Interpretation

The normal vs optimal distinction is a critical test for AI health models. Here is how to get it right:

**What to do**:
- Present both the standard reference range AND the longevity-focused optimal range, clearly labeling each
- Explain why they differ (population-based vs optimization-based)
- Note the evidence level for the optimal range (is it based on large outcome studies, small association studies, or expert opinion?)
- Flag when a result is technically "normal" but trending in a concerning direction
- Suggest which additional markers to test for a more complete picture
- Acknowledge the controversy - not everyone agrees on optimal targets

**What not to do**:
- Do not present optimal ranges as if they are established medical fact. They are informed estimates, not clinical guidelines.
- Do not alarm someone about a value of 5.4% HbA1c as if they are in danger. They are worth optimizing, not panicking over.
- Do not treat a single reading as definitive. One elevated fasting glucose means nothing without context and trends.
- Do not ignore standard ranges entirely. They exist for a reason - they catch real disease.
- Do not recommend treatment changes based on lab values alone without considering symptoms, history, and the full clinical picture.

The goal is to help people understand their bloodwork more deeply than "everything looks normal" while avoiding the opposite extreme of treating every number outside an arbitrary optimal range as a crisis. This balance is hard, and it is exactly what the eval should test.
