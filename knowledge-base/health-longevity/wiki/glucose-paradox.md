# Why Fasting Glucose Can Go UP During Health Optimization

A research-backed guide to the paradox where fasting glucose rises even as other health markers improve. This confuses a lot of people on low-carb diets, time-restricted eating, and longevity protocols. The short version: elevated fasting glucose in this context usually means something different than it does in metabolic disease.

---

## Physiological Insulin Resistance: Adaptive Glucose Sparing

When someone follows a low-carb or ketogenic diet for weeks to months, their muscles switch from burning glucose to burning fatty acids and ketones as their primary fuel. The muscles become less responsive to insulin - not because they are damaged (as in type 2 diabetes), but because they no longer need glucose [1][2].

This is called "adaptive glucose sparing" or "physiological insulin resistance." The body is conserving its limited glucose supply for the tissues that absolutely require it - primarily the brain and red blood cells [1].

Dr. Ted Naiman describes this as muscles entering "glucose refusal mode." On a long-term keto diet, muscles prefer fat as fuel, so they resist insulin's signal to take up glucose. The glucose stays in the bloodstream, and fasting glucose readings rise - often to 90-110 mg/dL in healthy, lean, low-carb athletes [2].

### How This Differs from Pathological Insulin Resistance

The distinction is critical:

- **Pathological insulin resistance**: High fasting glucose AND high fasting insulin. The body is producing extra insulin to force glucose into resistant cells. This is the hallmark of metabolic syndrome and pre-diabetes. HOMA-IR is elevated (above 2.0) [3].
- **Physiological insulin resistance**: Mildly elevated fasting glucose with LOW fasting insulin. The body is not struggling to control glucose - it is deliberately keeping glucose available for the brain. HOMA-IR remains low (below 1.5) [1][3].

The formula: HOMA-IR = (Fasting Insulin x Fasting Glucose) / 405, where insulin is in uIU/mL and glucose is in mg/dL. A HOMA-IR below 1.0 indicates optimal insulin sensitivity. Above 1.9 suggests early insulin resistance. Above 2.9 is significant insulin resistance [3].

Someone on a ketogenic diet with a fasting glucose of 100 mg/dL and fasting insulin of 3 uIU/mL has a HOMA-IR of 0.74 - well within the optimal range. That same glucose reading with a fasting insulin of 15 uIU/mL gives a HOMA-IR of 3.7 - a completely different situation.

### Reversibility

This adaptation reverses promptly when dietary carbohydrates are reintroduced. It is not a permanent metabolic change. A standard oral glucose tolerance test (OGTT) after several days of carbohydrate reintroduction typically shows normal glucose disposal [2].

Grandl et al. (2018) showed in a mouse study that short-term ketogenic feeding induced hepatic insulin resistance - the liver continued producing glucose even when insulin signaled it to stop. But this was driven by increased fat oxidation and metabolic adaptation, not by cellular damage. The animals appeared metabolically healthy in the fasted state; the glucose intolerance only showed up during a glucose challenge [4].


## The Dawn Phenomenon

### What Happens

Between approximately 4:00 AM and 9:00 AM, the body releases a surge of counter-regulatory hormones - cortisol, growth hormone, epinephrine, and norepinephrine. These hormones stimulate the liver to release glucose into the bloodstream, preparing the body for waking activity [5][6].

In healthy individuals, a corresponding insulin release keeps this in check. But in people with any degree of insulin resistance, or in those on low-carb diets where insulin levels are already low, the glucose release goes partially unchecked. Fasting glucose measured first thing in the morning can read 10-30 mg/dL higher than glucose measured at other times of day [5].

### Clinical Impact

A 2013 study of 248 people with non-insulin-treated diabetes found that the dawn phenomenon worsened overall glycemic control by approximately 0.4% on HbA1c and about 12 mg/dL in 24-hour mean glucose [6].

### The Cortisol Debate

Some researchers have found that blocking cortisol does not fully prevent the dawn effect, leading some to question cortisol's role as the primary driver [6]. The issue is not fully settled. Current consensus is that the dawn phenomenon results from circadian variation in hepatic glucose production - the liver's glucose output starts increasing in the evening, peaks toward the end of an overnight fast, and declines during daytime until a late afternoon low point [5].

### Practical Implications

If you measure fasting glucose at 6:00 AM, you may get a reading 15-25 mg/dL higher than if you measured at 10:00 AM after the cortisol peak has passed. This means a single morning fasting glucose reading can be misleading, especially for people already worried about their numbers.


## How Low-Carb and Time-Restricted Eating Raise Fasting Glucose

### Low-Carb / Ketogenic Diets

The mechanism is adaptive glucose sparing (described above). Elevated fasting glucose typically appears after several months of strict carbohydrate restriction. The body upregulates hepatic glucose production to maintain supply for glucose-dependent tissues while the rest of the body runs on fat and ketones [1][2].

A key study from the Journal of Physiology showed that ketogenic diet-fed mice exhibited decreased glucose tolerance compared to controls, but through a specific mechanism: blunted suppression of hepatic glucose production by insulin, not impaired tissue glucose uptake [4]. The muscles were fine at taking up glucose when given it - the liver just would not stop making it.

### Time-Restricted Eating

The research on time-restricted eating (TRE) and fasting glucose is more mixed. Meta-analyses of TRE studies show that it generally reduces fasting glucose and HbA1c in people with type 2 diabetes [7]. However, conflicting results exist:

- Some reviews show marked fasting glucose reduction after TRE
- Others find no significant effect on fasting glucose [7]
- The interaction between eating window timing, meal composition, and individual metabolic state makes it hard to isolate TRE's specific effect

The paradoxical glucose rise with TRE is most likely to occur when it is combined with low-carb eating, as the two interventions stack their effects on hepatic glucose output and insulin sensitivity.


## Metformin: Timing Matters

Metformin is widely used for blood sugar management and is increasingly popular in the longevity community. How you time it affects fasting glucose specifically.

### Evening vs Morning Dosing

A study of 258 diabetic patients found that 52.7% responded well to switching metformin to bedtime dosing. In patients on combined therapy, nighttime insulin requirements could be reduced by up to 70%, with no early morning hypoglycemia or morning hyperglycemia [8].

The logic: metformin taken at bedtime suppresses overnight hepatic glucose production - the exact mechanism behind the dawn phenomenon. Morning dosing misses this window entirely, acting instead on daytime glucose management.

### Delayed-Release Formulation

A randomized trial comparing delayed-release metformin found that morning dosing had 28% lower bioavailability compared with evening or twice-daily dosing, yet glucose-lowering effects were maintained [9]. This suggests that metformin's primary glucose-lowering mechanism operates in the gut (via GLP-1 enhancement), not just through systemic absorption.

### Pre-Breakfast Timing

A 2024 randomized crossover trial found that morning exercise combined with pre-breakfast metformin persistently reduced glucose more than morning exercise with post-breakfast metformin through six weeks of intervention [10]. The timing relationship between metformin, food, and activity all interact.


## The Three Glucose Measures: What Each Actually Tells You

### Fasting Glucose

A snapshot of blood glucose after 8-12 hours without food. Heavily influenced by:
- Dawn phenomenon (cortisol timing)
- Recent diet composition (low-carb raises it)
- Sleep quality the night before
- Stress and illness

A single fasting glucose reading is the least reliable of the three measures. It captures one moment and is subject to acute influences that may not reflect long-term metabolic health.

### HbA1c

Measures the percentage of hemoglobin that has been glycated (bound to glucose) over the previous 2-3 months. This is a rolling average, not a snapshot. It is less susceptible to day-to-day variation [11].

On a low-carb diet, HbA1c will almost always be lower than the fasting glucose reading would suggest [2]. This is because postprandial (after-meal) glucose spikes are minimized on low-carb eating, even if morning fasting glucose runs a bit high. The 24-hour glucose exposure is lower overall.

### Continuous Glucose Monitor (CGM) Data

CGMs measure interstitial glucose every 5-15 minutes, providing a full picture of glucose dynamics over days and weeks. Key metrics include:

- **Time in range** (70-140 mg/dL): the percentage of time glucose stays in a healthy band
- **Glucose variability**: standard deviation or coefficient of variation
- **Glucose Management Indicator (GMI)**: CGM-derived estimate of HbA1c

### The Discordance Problem

HbA1c and CGM-derived GMI frequently disagree. A real-world study found that only 11% of patients had HbA1c-GMI discordance less than 0.1%, while 50% had differences of 0.5% or more and 22% had differences of 1% or more [11]. Sources of discordance include red blood cell turnover rate, hemoglobin variants, chronic kidney or liver disease, medications, and pregnancy.

The practical takeaway: use all three measures together. If fasting glucose is 98 mg/dL but HbA1c is 5.0% and CGM shows time-in-range above 95%, the fasting glucose number is far less concerning than if HbA1c were 5.6% and CGM showed frequent spikes to 160 mg/dL.


## When Elevated Fasting Glucose Is Benign vs Concerning

### Likely Benign

Elevated fasting glucose is probably adaptive and not pathological when:

- Fasting insulin is low (below 5 uIU/mL) and HOMA-IR is below 1.5 [1][3]
- HbA1c is in the optimal range (4.8-5.2%) [2]
- The person is on a low-carb or ketogenic diet
- CGM data shows low glucose variability and high time-in-range
- Other metabolic markers are improving (triglycerides down, HDL up, waist circumference down)
- The elevation is mild (90-105 mg/dL, not 120+)

### Concerning

Elevated fasting glucose warrants investigation when:

- Fasting insulin is also elevated (above 8-10 uIU/mL)
- HOMA-IR is above 2.0 [3]
- HbA1c is trending upward (above 5.5% or rising over sequential tests)
- Triglyceride/HDL ratio is above 2.0 (a surrogate marker for insulin resistance)
- The person is not on a low-carb diet and has no clear physiological reason for the elevation
- Other inflammatory markers (hsCRP) are elevated
- There is a family history of type 2 diabetes


## Cortisol Elevation from Over-Optimization

### The Paradox of Rigid Protocol Adherence

Aggressive health optimization - strict calorie restriction, intense exercise, rigid meal timing, frequent supplement dosing, obsessive biomarker tracking - can itself become a stressor that elevates cortisol [12][13].

Research on overtraining syndrome (OTS) shows that excessive physical activity without adequate recovery leads to hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis dysregulation. The hormonal signature is complex: during overreaching, cortisol response is blunted while ACTH is augmented. In full overtraining, both ACTH and cortisol responses decrease [12].

Sleep restriction compounds the problem. Studies show that inadequate sleep in athletes elevates cortisol, impairs muscle glycogen restoration, increases lactate accumulation, and alters heart rate variability [13]. All of these effects raise fasting glucose.

### The Stress-Glucose Connection

Acute psychophysical stress increases cortisol by approximately 23% (from 8.6 to 10.6 ug/dL at 30 minutes) and directly raises blood glucose through hepatic glucose release [14]. Chronic stress maintains this elevation.

The irony: someone following an aggressive health protocol that causes psychological stress, sleep disruption, or overtraining may see their fasting glucose rise precisely because of the protocol, not despite it.


## Reading Glucose in Context of Other Improving Biomarkers

### The Multi-Marker Framework

HRV, resting heart rate, and glucose are connected through the autonomic nervous system. Research from the Cardiovascular Health Study found that diminished HRV is independently associated with metabolic syndrome, regardless of fasting glucose levels [15]. The Framingham Heart Study confirmed that both LF and HF power (HRV components) are reduced in those with diabetes or impaired fasting glucose [16].

The practical question: if HRV is improving, resting heart rate is declining, sleep quality is up, triglycerides are down, and fasting glucose is slightly elevated - is glucose the real concern?

Usually not. The constellation of improving markers suggests the metabolic trajectory is positive. The isolated glucose reading is likely explained by:
- Adaptive glucose sparing from dietary changes
- Dawn phenomenon timing
- Measurement variability

### What to Track Together

For a complete picture, monitor these as a panel rather than fixating on any single number:

1. **Fasting glucose** - context-dependent, least reliable alone
2. **Fasting insulin** - reveals what glucose hides (the metabolic effort behind the number)
3. **HbA1c** - 2-3 month average, less susceptible to acute effects
4. **HOMA-IR** - the glucose-insulin relationship is more informative than either alone
5. **Triglyceride/HDL ratio** - cheap insulin resistance surrogate from standard lipid panel
6. **HRV trend** - autonomic nervous system health, inversely associated with metabolic dysfunction [15]
7. **Resting heart rate trend** - declining RHR with improving fitness suggests positive trajectory
8. **hsCRP** - systemic inflammation, which independently drives glucose elevation

A rising fasting glucose accompanied by improving insulin, HbA1c, HRV, triglycerides, and inflammatory markers is a fundamentally different situation from a rising fasting glucose accompanied by rising insulin, worsening HRV, and increasing inflammation.

---

## Key Takeaways

1. Elevated fasting glucose on a low-carb diet usually reflects adaptive glucose sparing - the body conserving glucose for the brain while muscles run on fat. Check fasting insulin and HOMA-IR before worrying.

2. The dawn phenomenon can add 10-30 mg/dL to morning glucose readings due to cortisol-driven hepatic glucose output. Time of measurement matters.

3. HbA1c is a better indicator than fasting glucose for long-term metabolic health. On low-carb diets, HbA1c is almost always lower than fasting glucose would predict.

4. Metformin timing matters. Evening dosing suppresses overnight hepatic glucose production and targets the dawn phenomenon directly.

5. Over-optimization (excessive exercise, sleep restriction, rigid protocols, psychological stress) can elevate cortisol and raise fasting glucose. The protocol itself can become the problem.

6. Never interpret glucose in isolation. Use the full panel: glucose + insulin + HbA1c + HOMA-IR + TG/HDL ratio + HRV + hsCRP. If everything else is improving and fasting glucose is slightly up, the glucose reading is probably the least important number on your panel.
