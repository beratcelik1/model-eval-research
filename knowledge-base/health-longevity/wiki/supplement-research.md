# Supplement Research - What the Evidence Actually Says

Research notes on major longevity supplements, their actual evidence levels, and how an AI model should handle the gap between "promising" and "proven."

---

## The Evidence Hierarchy

Not all studies are created equal. When evaluating supplement claims, the strength of evidence matters enormously.

1. **Systematic reviews / meta-analyses of RCTs** - the gold standard. Pool results from multiple randomized controlled trials.
2. **Large RCTs (randomized controlled trials)** - controlled, blinded, placebo-matched. The bigger and longer, the better.
3. **Small RCTs** - same design but with 20-60 participants and 6-12 week durations. Most supplement research lives here.
4. **Cohort / observational studies** - track large groups over time. Can show correlations, cannot prove causation.
5. **Case reports / case series** - descriptions of individual outcomes. Useful for generating hypotheses, not for proving them.
6. **Animal studies (mostly mice)** - helpful for understanding mechanisms. Terrible at predicting human outcomes. Most mouse results do not translate.
7. **In vitro (cell culture)** - shows what is biochemically possible. Says nothing about what happens in a living human body.

The supplement industry routinely markets products based on animal and in vitro data. When you see "clinically studied," check whether the study involved humans, how many, for how long, and who funded it.


## Major Longevity Supplements

### NMN / NR (NAD+ Precursors)

**What they claim to do**: Raise NAD+ levels, which decline with age. Higher NAD+ is associated with better cellular energy production and DNA repair.

**What the human evidence shows**:

- Both NMN and NR reliably raise blood NAD+ levels in humans. This is established across multiple trials.
- A 2024 systematic review and meta-analysis of NMN RCTs found significant elevation of blood NAD levels, but most clinically relevant outcomes (strength, body composition, metabolic markers) were not significantly different from placebo.
- A 2025 meta-analysis found NMN showed no significant effects on skeletal muscle index, handgrip strength, gait speed, or chair-stand time in older adults.
- The largest NMN trial to date (Yoshino et al., 2021) had only 25 postmenopausal women with prediabetes and showed improved muscle insulin sensitivity at 250 mg/day. That is the strongest human outcome result so far.
- A February 2026 RCT found 1,200 mg/day NMN reduced inflammatory signals after intense exercise, but it also abolished the 171% increase in muscle mitochondria that normally occurs with exercise. This is a significant concern - it may blunt exercise adaptation.
- A landmark June 2025 NR trial in Werner syndrome (premature aging) patients showed NR safely boosted NAD+ and improved cardiovascular markers and skin ulcers. But Werner syndrome is a rare genetic condition, not normal aging.
- Neither NMN nor NR has demonstrated meaningful anti-aging effects in healthy humans in any trial.

**Evidence level**: Small RCTs with surrogate endpoints. Promising mechanism, unproven clinical benefits.

**FDA status**: As of September 2025, the FDA reversed its earlier position and confirmed NMN is not excluded from the definition of a dietary supplement.

### Berberine

**What it claims to do**: Lower blood sugar, improve lipid profiles, act as a "natural metformin."

**What the human evidence shows**:

- A 2025 meta-analysis of 46 clinical studies found berberine and metformin equally effective at lowering blood glucose, with berberine performing slightly better on HbA1c, fasting plasma glucose, and postprandial glucose.
- In one randomized trial of prediabetic patients, berberine reduced fasting plasma glucose by 12.6 mg/dL vs metformin's 10.8 mg/dL.
- Berberine may be better than metformin for lipids specifically - triglycerides and total cholesterol were significantly lower in berberine groups by week 13 in one trial.
- GI side effects occurred in 20% of berberine recipients vs 30% for metformin.
- A 2025 Frontiers meta-analysis confirmed berberine improved components of metabolic syndrome in placebo-controlled RCTs.

**Important caveats**: Most berberine studies are small (36-90 participants), short-term (8-16 weeks), and conducted predominantly in Chinese populations. Many have high risk of bias. Metformin, by contrast, has been studied in hundreds of large, long-term, multi-ethnic trials. Calling berberine "nature's metformin" dramatically overstates the evidence base.

**Evidence level**: Multiple small RCTs. Real metabolic effects, but the evidence base is thin compared to pharmaceutical alternatives.

### Omega-3 Fatty Acids (EPA/DHA)

**What they claim to do**: Reduce cardiovascular risk, lower inflammation, support brain health.

**What the human evidence shows**:

- The VITAL trial (25,871 participants, 5.3 years follow-up) is the largest primary prevention omega-3 study. At 840 mg/day (EPA+DHA), it found no statistically significant reduction in the primary composite endpoint of major cardiovascular events.
- However, secondary outcomes were notable: 28% reduction in heart attack risk, 50% reduction in fatal heart attack risk. No benefit for stroke.
- Subgroup finding: participants with low fish intake (<1.5 servings/week) saw a 19% reduction in major cardiovascular events and 40% reduction in heart attacks. African Americans also showed consistent benefits.
- EPA-only formulations may perform better than EPA+DHA combinations. The REDUCE-IT trial (4g icosapent ethyl, a purified EPA) showed 25% relative risk reduction in cardiovascular events.
- The dose matters. Most positive trials use 2-4g/day. The typical supplement dose of 1g/day may be insufficient for cardiovascular benefit.

**Evidence level**: Large RCTs. Moderate evidence for cardiovascular benefit at adequate doses, especially in people with low fish intake.

### Creatine

**What it claims to do**: Beyond muscle performance - cognitive benefits, neuroprotection, cellular energy.

**What the human evidence shows**:

- A 2024 systematic review and meta-analysis of 16 RCTs (492 participants) found creatine supplementation improved memory, attention time, and processing speed.
- Subgroup analyses: benefits were more pronounced in people with diseases, ages 18-60, and in females.
- A 2025 pilot study in 19 Alzheimer's patients (20g/day for 8 weeks) showed 11% increase in brain creatine levels and moderate improvements in working memory and executive function.
- A 2024 study found a single dose of creatine improved cognitive performance and restored phosphate levels during sleep deprivation.
- The European Food Safety Authority (EFSA) evaluated a health claim for creatine and cognition in 2024, noting the evidence is suggestive but not yet conclusive.

**Evidence level**: Small RCTs showing real cognitive effects. The muscle/strength evidence (not covered here) is very strong with decades of large trials. Cognitive benefits are newer and less established.

### Ashwagandha (KSM-66)

**What it claims to do**: Reduce cortisol, improve sleep, lower stress and anxiety.

**What the human evidence shows**:

- A 60-day double-blind RCT (N=64, chronic stress) found 600 mg/day ashwagandha root extract reduced stress scores by 72.3% on GHQ-28, 71.6% on DASS, and cortisol levels by 27.9% vs 7.9% for placebo, and stress scores by 44% on PSS. These are large effect sizes for a supplement.
- An 80-person RCT of KSM-66 showed improved sleep quality, reduced sleep onset latency, and better mental alertness on rising, with benefits most pronounced at 600 mg/day for 8+ weeks.
- A 2025 12-month safety and efficacy study (N=191) found cortisol levels modestly declined, quality of life improved significantly, and 68.7% showed clinical improvement. Participants 50+ benefited most.
- A 24-week study showed 600 mg/day reduced stress, food cravings, cortisol, BMI, and body weight vs placebo.
- The 12-month study found no deleterious effects on hepatic, renal, or thyroid function, with only 9.4% incidence of mild adverse events.

**Evidence level**: Multiple RCTs with consistent results. Among the better-studied herbal supplements. The cortisol and stress reduction evidence is reasonably solid by supplement standards.

### Magnesium

**What it claims to do**: Depends on the form. Sleep, cognition, relaxation, bowel regularity, muscle function.

**Different forms and their evidence**:

- **Magnesium glycinate** - Best for sleep and relaxation. A 2025 double-blind RCT (N=155) found 250 mg elemental magnesium from bisglycinate improved Insomnia Severity Index scores significantly (intervention -3.9 vs placebo -2.3, p=0.049, Cohen's d=0.2) after 4 weeks. Most improvement appeared within 14 days. Gentlest on the GI tract.
- **Magnesium L-threonate** - Marketed for brain health due to blood-brain barrier crossing. A 2025 RCT found 6 weeks improved hand-eye coordination, reaction time, and deep/REM sleep quality. Still limited evidence - mostly one research group (Bhatt lab at MIT originally).
- **Magnesium citrate** - Highly bioavailable, well-absorbed. A 2025 animal study found it selectively elevated hippocampal BDNF. More commonly used for general deficiency and bowel regularity due to mild laxative effect.
- **Magnesium oxide** - Cheapest form, worst absorption (roughly 4%). Primarily useful as a laxative.

**The bigger picture**: Roughly 50% of Americans consume less than the recommended daily allowance of magnesium. Supplementation makes sense for many people purely on a deficiency basis, before considering any "optimization" angle. The form matters for the use case.

**Evidence level**: Varies by form. General magnesium sufficiency is well-supported. Specific form-based claims are supported by small RCTs.

### NAC (N-Acetylcysteine)

**What it claims to do**: Boost glutathione (the body's master antioxidant), support respiratory and liver health.

**What the human evidence shows**:

- NAC is the standard-of-care treatment for acetaminophen (Tylenol) overdose. It is nearly 100% effective at preventing liver damage if given within 8 hours. This is not a supplement claim - this is FDA-approved medicine.
- For respiratory health: NAC reduces inflammation in airways and may reduce COPD exacerbation frequency, but results are mixed across trials. The clinical efficacy lags behind the preclinical data.
- For liver function beyond overdose: a meta-analysis found NAC significantly increased albumin and decreased bilirubin, but showed no significant effect on AST, ALT, or ALP. The evidence for general "liver support" is modest.
- NAC reliably increases intracellular glutathione. Whether this translates to clinical benefits in healthy people is less clear.
- At oral doses of 600-1800 mg/day, NAC is generally well-tolerated. Some GI discomfort at higher doses.

**Evidence level**: Strong for acetaminophen toxicity (this is established medicine). Moderate for COPD. Weak-to-moderate for general antioxidant and liver health claims.

### Vitamin D

**What it claims to do**: Everything from bone health to immune function to cancer prevention to mood.

**The optimal levels debate**:

- **Institute of Medicine (IOM)**: 20 ng/mL (50 nmol/L) is adequate for 97.5% of the population
- **Endocrine Society (2011)**: targeted >30 ng/mL. Their 2024 updated statement walked this back significantly - they no longer endorse a specific optimal target and stated that "25(OH)D levels that provide outcome-specific benefits have not been established in clinical trials."
- **Vitamin D Council / functional medicine**: targets 40-80 ng/mL
- **Longevity community**: often targets 50-80 ng/mL

Research suggests the optimal level may depend on the outcome. A 2025 dose-response meta-analysis found that for metabolic diseases, higher levels (50+ ng/mL / 125+ nmol/L) were associated with significantly lower diabetes risk. For bone health, 20-30 ng/mL may be sufficient.

**Dosing**: The Endocrine Society recommends 50,000 IU weekly for 8 weeks to correct deficiency, followed by 1,500-2,000 IU daily maintenance. Many longevity practitioners use 4,000-5,000 IU daily.

**Evidence level**: Strong for bone health and deficiency correction. The VITAL trial (25,871 people) found no significant cancer or cardiovascular benefit from 2,000 IU/day vitamin D. Higher-dose trials are limited.


## Common Supplement Interactions That Matter

- **Blood thinners + fish oil / vitamin E / ginkgo**: Increased bleeding risk. Fish oil at high doses (>3g/day) combined with warfarin can push INR to dangerous levels.
- **Calcium / iron + thyroid medication (levothyroxine)**: Calcium and iron can reduce thyroid med absorption by up to 50% if taken within 4 hours.
- **Magnesium + fluoroquinolone antibiotics (cipro, levaquin)**: Magnesium can bind these antibiotics and block up to 90% of absorption.
- **St. John's Wort + almost anything**: Induces liver enzymes that accelerate drug metabolism. Can reduce effectiveness of birth control, blood thinners, immunosuppressants, and antidepressants.
- **5-HTP + SSRIs/SNRIs**: Risk of serotonin syndrome. This is a potentially fatal interaction.
- **Berberine + metformin**: Both lower blood sugar. Combined use increases hypoglycemia risk. Berberine also inhibits CYP enzymes and may alter drug metabolism.
- **High-dose vitamin D without K2**: May promote calcium deposition in arteries rather than bones. The evidence for this is debated but plausible based on the biochemistry.

Nearly 70% of patients on prescription drugs do not tell their doctor about supplement use. This is a serious safety gap.


## The Problem with Supplement Research

**Industry funding**: Most supplement trials are funded by the companies selling the products. This creates systematic bias toward positive results. Publication bias compounds the problem - negative results are less likely to be published.

**Small sample sizes**: The typical supplement trial has 20-80 participants. Pharmaceutical trials routinely enroll thousands. Small samples amplify noise and make it easy to find "statistically significant" results that don't replicate.

**Short durations**: Most supplement trials run 4-16 weeks. Longevity is a decades-long process. A 12-week trial showing a surrogate marker moved slightly tells you almost nothing about whether the supplement will affect how long someone lives.

**Surrogate endpoints**: Raising NAD+ blood levels, lowering cortisol by 10%, or improving a sleep questionnaire score are surrogate endpoints. They may or may not translate to actual health outcomes. The history of medicine is full of surrogate endpoints that moved in the right direction while the patients got sicker.

**Replication**: Many supplement findings have not been independently replicated. A single positive study from the supplement manufacturer is not sufficient evidence.


## How an AI Model Should Handle This

The gap between "promising research" and "proven" is the most important thing an AI model needs to get right in supplement discussions.

**What to do**:
- Always state the evidence level honestly. "There are a few small RCTs showing X" is very different from "large trials have established X."
- Distinguish between "this supplement raises a biomarker" and "this supplement improves health outcomes."
- Flag industry funding and conflicts of interest when relevant.
- Differentiate populations - results in diabetic patients may not apply to healthy people.
- When someone asks "does X work?" - the honest answer for most supplements is "there are signals suggesting it might help with Y, but the evidence is preliminary."

**What not to do**:
- Don't present animal studies as if they predict human outcomes.
- Don't treat a supplement that moved a biomarker as "proven to work."
- Don't dismiss all supplements as worthless - some (magnesium, omega-3, vitamin D, creatine) have substantial evidence for specific uses.
- Don't recommend specific supplements without noting that individual response varies and interactions with medications matter.
- Don't say "consult your doctor" as a way to avoid the question. Most doctors know very little about supplements. Give the information and note the limitations.

The goal is calibrated honesty - not hype, not dismissal, but an accurate picture of what we know and what we don't.
