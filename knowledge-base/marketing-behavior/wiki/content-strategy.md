# Content Strategy and What Makes Content Convert

Research notes on why content spreads, what works on X/Twitter, and the gap between "good content" and content that drives action.

---

## The Science of Viral Content

### Jonah Berger's STEPPS Framework

Berger spent years at Wharton studying why certain ideas spread. His 2013 book "Contagious: Why Things Catch On" distilled the research into six factors. The acronym is STEPPS:

1. **Social Currency** - People share things that make them look good. If your content makes someone seem smart, funny, or in-the-know, they'll pass it along. This is why "insider knowledge" framing works so well. The word "secret" creates instant curiosity because it implies exclusive access.

2. **Triggers** - "Top of mind, tip of tongue." Content tied to everyday cues gets talked about more. Kit Kat ran a campaign linking their product to coffee breaks - not because coffee is exciting, but because people drink coffee every day. Frequency of trigger matters more than intensity of emotion.

3. **Emotion** - When we care, we share. But not all emotions are equal. High-arousal emotions (awe, anger, anxiety, excitement) drive sharing. Low-arousal emotions (sadness, contentment) suppress it. The key distinction is physiological arousal, not valence. Angry content and awe-inspiring content both spread - calm content does not.

4. **Public** - Built to show, built to grow. If people can see others doing something, they're more likely to imitate it. Apple putting their logo on the outside of laptops (facing viewers, not the user) is the textbook example. Movember works because mustaches are visible.

5. **Practical Value** - News you can use. People share useful information because it helps others and makes the sharer look helpful. "5 things I learned about X" posts perform well because they package practical value in a shareable format.

6. **Stories** - Information travels under the guise of narrative. People don't share data points - they share stories that contain data points. The carrier narrative matters as much as the payload.

### What the Research Actually Shows

Berger's work is grounded in quantitative analysis of hundreds of campaigns and content pieces. The key finding is that virality is not random luck - it follows patterns. Content that hits multiple STEPPS factors spreads more reliably than content that hits one or none.

But there's a catch. Viral spread and commercial value are different things. Content can go massively viral and drive zero revenue. The STEPPS framework explains sharing, not conversion. This distinction matters for AI evals - we need to test both.


## What Works on X/Twitter

### Content Format Performance (2024-2025 Data)

The data from Metricool's 2024 X study and various social media benchmarks shows clear format preferences:

- **Text posts** - 0.48% average engagement rate. Still the backbone of X. Short, punchy text with a strong opinion or insight outperforms everything else on a per-effort basis.
- **Link posts** - 0.13% engagement rate. Significantly worse. The algorithm deprioritizes external links because X wants people to stay on the platform.
- **Image posts** - Sit between text and link posts. Images grab attention in the feed but don't consistently outperform strong text.
- **Video posts** - X has been pushing video hard, but engagement data is mixed. Short-form video (under 60 seconds) does well. Longer video struggles.
- **GIF posts** - Surprisingly, GIFs get the most median interactions (6.5 per post). They stop the scroll and add personality without the production overhead of video.
- **Threads** - Average 2-4% engagement vs 0.5-1.5% for single tweets. Threads are the highest-performing format for most niches. They reward depth and keep people on the platform.

### Thread Strategy

Threads work because they create an open loop. You read the first tweet, you want to know what comes next. The structure that performs best:

- **Hook tweet** - The single most important element. It has to stop the scroll in under 2 seconds. Strong hooks use: a surprising stat, a contrarian take, a bold claim, or a relatable problem.
- **Body tweets** - Each tweet should deliver one idea. Short sentences. White space. The reader should feel momentum, not density.
- **Closing tweet** - Summarize the thread, restate the core insight, and include a CTA (follow, retweet, reply).

The best-performing threads share a few traits: they teach something specific, they use concrete examples instead of abstractions, and they are written at a 6th-grade reading level even when the topic is sophisticated.

### Hook Formulas That Work

Research from Copy Hackers and various copywriting practitioners points to several reliable hook structures:

- **The "I spent X doing Y, here's what I learned"** - Implies earned authority and practical value
- **Contrarian opener** - "Most people think X. They're wrong." Creates tension
- **Specific number + surprising claim** - "I analyzed 10,000 tweets. 94% of viral ones did this."
- **Open loop question** - "What if everything you know about [topic] is backwards?"
- **Personal vulnerability** - "I lost $50K because I didn't understand this" - combines Social Currency (insider knowledge) with Emotion

The common thread: pattern interruption. The feed is full of predictable content. Hooks work by breaking the pattern.


## Engagement Metrics: What Actually Matters

### Vanity Metrics vs Actionable Metrics

The social media industry has a measurement problem. Most of the numbers people obsess over don't connect to business outcomes.

**Vanity metrics (look good, mean little):**
- Follower count - grows naturally over time, doesn't reflect quality
- Impressions - how many times content appeared, not how many people cared
- Likes - lowest-effort engagement, weakest signal of real interest
- Raw view counts - especially on video, where "view" can mean 3 seconds of autoplay

**Actionable metrics (harder to inflate, actually matter):**
- Engagement rate - percentage of viewers who interacted meaningfully
- Saves/bookmarks - someone found this valuable enough to return to later
- Shares/retweets - "I want my audience to see this" is a much stronger endorsement than a like
- Click-through rate - did the content drive someone to take a next step?
- Reply depth - are people having real conversations, or just dropping emoji?
- Profile visits from content - did this make someone curious about who you are?
- Follower growth rate - not total followers, but the rate of change

The fundamental question: does this metric help us decide what to do differently? If a number goes up or down and it doesn't change your strategy, it's a vanity metric.

### X-Specific Metrics in 2025

Average engagement rate per post from an X influencer in 2025 was 0.39%, up significantly from 0.09% the prior year. This jump coincides with X's algorithm changes that boosted content from engaged accounts.

Average post interactions: 32.89 likes, 6.67 retweets, 2.56 replies. The ratio matters. A post with lots of likes but few retweets reached people who liked it but didn't think it was worth sharing. A post with high retweet-to-like ratio is genuinely spreading.


## The Gap: Good Content vs Content That Drives Action

### Where Most Content Fails

There's a massive gap between content that people enjoy and content that changes behavior. You can get 10,000 likes on a post and drive zero sales, zero signups, zero meaningful action.

The gap exists because most content optimizes for consumption, not conversion. It entertains without directing. It informs without motivating. It resonates without moving.

Research on emotional engagement and conversion shows that emotionally connected customers have a 306% higher lifetime value than merely satisfied customers. But emotional connection and emotional reaction are different things. A funny tweet creates a reaction. A tweet that makes someone feel understood creates a connection.

### What Bridges the Gap

Content that converts typically does these things:

1. **Names the reader's specific situation** - Not "entrepreneurs" but "solo founders who just hit $10K MRR and don't know what to do next"
2. **Creates a felt need** - PAS (Problem-Agitate-Solve) works because it makes the problem feel urgent before presenting the solution
3. **Provides a clear next step** - One CTA, not three. "Reply with X" or "Click the link in bio" - never both
4. **Builds trust over time** - The first piece of content rarely converts. The 10th does. Consistency builds familiarity, and familiarity builds trust
5. **Matches the audience's readiness** - Content for problem-aware audiences looks different from content for solution-aware audiences


## X Growth Case Studies and Patterns

### What Rapidly Growing Accounts Have in Common

Analyzing accounts that grew significantly on X in 2024-2025 reveals consistent patterns:

**Posting frequency** - The data is clear: higher posting frequency correlates with larger account size. The average number of weekly posts jumped from 15.97 in 2024 to 17.34 in 2025. But frequency alone isn't enough - it has to be frequency plus quality.

**Consistency over optimization** - Posting 3x a day at random times beats posting once a day at the "optimal" time. The algorithm rewards accounts that show up reliably. Accounts with the most followers tend to post the most long-form content and share the most threads.

**Community engagement** - Rapid growth accounts don't just broadcast. They reply to other creators, participate in conversations, and build real relationships. The algorithm notices reciprocal engagement.

**Niche specificity** - Accounts that grow fastest tend to own a specific topic rather than being generalists. "The AI tools guy" grows faster than "tech commentator."

**Best posting times** - Tuesday through Thursday, 9 AM to 12 PM EST consistently shows the highest engagement. Wednesday is the single best day. Saturday is the worst. But these are averages across accounts - your specific audience may differ.

**Tweet lifespan** - Most engagement happens within the first 18 minutes of posting. This is why frequency matters so much. Each post is a short-lived opportunity.

### Growth Rates

Accounts using strategic content planning see 15-25% monthly follower growth. That compounds fast: 20% monthly growth turns 1,000 followers into 8,900 in a year. But most accounts plateau because they stop being strategic once they find a formula that "works okay."


## AI Content vs Human Content: What the Data Says

### A/B Test Results

This is where the research gets interesting for AI evals. Several studies have now compared AI-generated and human-written content head-to-head:

**SEO Performance:**
- AI content ranked lower in 21 of 25 tests in Semrush's 2024 study
- 57% of AI articles vs 58% of human articles reached Google's top 10 - nearly identical at the surface level
- But pure AI content (no human editing) only reached the top 3 in 6% of cases
- AI content edited by humans achieved 80% first-page rankings vs 22% for human-only content

**Advertising Performance:**
- Human-written Google ads got 45.41% more impressions than AI ads
- Humans achieved 4.98% CTR vs AI's 3.65% CTR
- The gap is significant but not enormous

**Traffic and Engagement:**
- Human-written content generates 5.44x more traffic over five months
- Human content leads to 41% longer session durations
- AI content without human editing had 18% higher bounce rates

**The Hybrid Finding:**
The most important data point: AI content edited by humans outperforms both pure AI and pure human content. The 80% first-page ranking rate for human-edited AI content vs 22% for human-only suggests that the combination is stronger than either alone.

### What This Means for AI Evals

Pure AI content underperforms human content on business metrics. But AI content with human editing outperforms human-only content. This suggests the bottleneck isn't generation quality - it's judgment. AI can produce good raw material, but it can't (yet) evaluate whether that material will resonate with a specific audience.

This is exactly the gap an eval should target: can the model make the judgment calls that turn acceptable content into effective content?


## Timing, Frequency, and Consistency

### The Data on Timing

Best times to post on X (composite from multiple 2025 studies):
- **Best window:** Tuesday through Thursday, 9 AM - 12 PM EST
- **Best single slot:** Wednesday at 9 AM EST
- **Expanded window:** Tuesday through Thursday, 12 PM - 6 PM also works well
- **Worst day:** Saturday - lowest engagement across all metrics
- **Weekend pattern:** Sunday outperforms Saturday but still lags weekdays

### Frequency Benchmarks

- 3-5 tweets per day is the standard recommendation for growth
- 1-3 per day gives stronger per-post performance (quality wins)
- Above 5 per day shows diminishing returns for most accounts
- Large accounts (50K+ followers) often post less frequently because each post already reaches a large audience
- The sweet spot depends on your content quality threshold - never sacrifice quality for frequency

### Why Consistency Beats Optimization

The algorithm rewards consistent posting patterns. An account that posts 3x daily for 30 days straight will outperform an account that posts 10x on Monday and 0x the rest of the week, even if total post volume is similar.

Consistency also builds audience habits. Your followers learn when to expect your content and start looking for it. This creates a compounding effect where each post performs a little better than the last because more people are primed to engage.

### Implications for AI Content Strategy

If an AI system is generating content for X, it needs to:
- Produce at a cadence of 3-5 posts per day
- Vary content types (text, threads, images) to avoid format fatigue
- Maintain a consistent voice across all outputs
- Optimize posting times per the audience, not per generic benchmarks
- Track which content types and topics are gaining traction and adjust

The question for evals: can the model maintain quality at the frequency the platform demands? Most AI content systems produce great first drafts but struggle with the volume needed for sustained X presence without becoming repetitive.

---

## Key Takeaways for Eval Design

1. **Virality and conversion are different skills.** A model might produce highly shareable content that drives zero action, or boring-looking content that converts well. Evals need to test both dimensions separately.

2. **Format matters as much as content.** The same message formatted as a thread vs a single tweet can have 4x different engagement. A model needs to know which format to use when.

3. **Hooks are testable.** We can evaluate whether a model produces hooks that create pattern interruption, open loops, and specificity - all measurable qualities.

4. **The hybrid model is strongest.** AI + human editing beats either alone. Evals should test whether the model can produce content that's close enough to final that a human editor can quickly refine it, rather than testing whether it produces publication-ready content.

5. **Consistency compounds.** Any eval of content strategy needs to test sustained quality over volume, not just single-output excellence.
